## Technical Deep-Dive

Search-time contamination (STC) is structurally unlike any contamination failure mode addressed by existing LLM evaluation practice, and the standard defenses do not apply [1]. In the static benchmark setting, contamination is a training-data problem: a model may have seen test questions during pretraining. Mitigations — periodic benchmark rotation, train/test split discipline, held-out test sets — work because contamination is fixed at training time and does not recur. In the web-searching research agent setting, contamination is an inference-time problem caused by the agent's own retrieval tools, and it recurs on every evaluation run against any publicly indexed benchmark [1].

The NTU and Alibaba researchers classify three contamination types ordered by severity [1]. Metadata leakage is the subtlest: the agent retrieves content about a benchmark question's origin — dataset name, task category, originating paper — that implicitly constrains the answer space without surfacing the answer directly. Question-context leakage occurs when the agent finds near-duplicate phrasings of the test question accompanied by revealing discussion, worked examples, or partial solutions in community forums or academic commentary. Explicit answer leakage is the most severe: the agent retrieves the gold label directly from a HuggingFace dataset page, a benchmark paper's evaluation table, or a publicly visible leaderboard submission. The researchers measured up to 4 percentage points of aggregate inflation across six benchmarks [1]. That figure is almost certainly a lower bound, because it reflects naturally occurring contamination in standard evaluation runs — an agent that optimized its retrieval queries to maximize benchmark leakage could achieve substantially higher inflation.

For enterprise teams evaluating or procuring research agents, the operational implication is precise: any benchmark whose test questions and gold labels appear in publicly indexed repositories is no longer a valid evaluation surface for web-augmented agents. This includes all major public agent benchmarks to the extent that their test cases appear in academic papers, community discussion, or dataset hosting platforms. The contamination risk scales with the benchmark's visibility — the most widely cited benchmarks are the most heavily indexed and therefore the most contaminated. This extends the benchmark integrity problems documented in recent briefs into a new layer: not just how scores are reported or whether task success reflects reasoning quality, but whether the scores are measuring reasoning at all rather than retrieval of pre-indexed answers.

The actionable controls follow directly from the failure mode [1]. Private evaluation corpora that are not publicly indexed become a prerequisite for valid research agent benchmarking — the same data-classification principle that governs proprietary model testing in regulated financial environments. Retrieval traces must be treated as first-class evaluation outputs: every tool call and retrieved document from an evaluation run should be cross-referenced against known benchmark corpora before a score is reported, a process that the execution provenance framework proposed in [6] is designed to support. Finally, search-time contamination risk should appear as a required disclosure dimension in any vendor-provided benchmark claim for research agents, alongside scaffold, sampling configuration, and inference cost disclosures that recent audits of agent benchmark papers have identified as routinely absent.

## Landscape Trends

- **Agent evaluation integrity is accumulating into a compound problem with no single fix, reinforcing the 2026-05-29 pattern.** The STC finding [1] adds a third structurally independent failure mode to a growing stack that already included trajectory reporting gaps and reward-hacking inflation across prior evaluation work, and now benchmark retrieval contamination. These failure modes are orthogonal — fixing disclosure discipline does not prevent reward hacking, and neither prevents inference-time retrieval of answers. Enterprise teams now need a layered evaluation defense — private corpora, retrieval-trace auditing, process-level scoring alongside task-success metrics, and inference-cost indexing — as minimum defensibility for any research agent procurement or capability assessment.

- **[Agentic Systems × Safety, Assurance & Governance]** Output-level compliance checking is being displaced by process-level trajectory governance across two independent research lines. TRACE [4] targets temporal assembly of malicious intent through individually benign actions; MAC-Bench [5] surfaces Goodhart-Law failures where agents optimize for reward by strategically violating procedural safety rules. Both findings converge with recent Safety brief work showing that evasion attacks frequently bypass frontier monitors, and with assessments identifying inter-agent communication opacity as among the most severe emerging oversight threats. The operational implication across all three research streams is consistent: binary output sampling is insufficient; full-trajectory inspection against procedural constraints is becoming the required enterprise governance baseline.

- **Tool interface design is shifting from semantic relevance to causal state-scoping, with direct implications for MCP tool annotation.** ToolChoiceConfusion [3] demonstrates empirically that exposing semantically relevant tools produces the same wrong-tool calls and premature actions as exposing all tools, because relevance is insufficient — a tool may be semantically related to a task yet causally unnecessary at the current execution step. This reframes the value of MCP tool annotations: current properties (read-only, destructive, idempotent) describe side-effect profiles but do not encode precondition-effect contracts that a causal filter requires. A typed dependency graph approach to skill-level causal scoping addresses an analogous problem at the skill level. Both signal a convergence from static tool menus toward dynamically scoped, state-dependent interfaces as the production reliability standard for multi-step agent deployments.

- **[Agentic Systems × LLM Production Infrastructure]** Memory evaluation and execution harness design are emerging as operationally coupled concerns that benchmarks continue to evaluate in isolation. LongMemEval-V2 [2] establishes that production agents in specialized environments must internalize interface affordances, multi-step workflow procedures, and recurring failure modes — not just user preference history — across sessions. This maps directly onto the harness substrate layer, where prior work has shown evaluation harness configuration can produce significant outcome variance when varied independently of model and task. The coupling is non-trivial in regulated financial deployments: an agent that cannot recall that a proprietary back-office system requires two-phase confirmation will fail regardless of base model capability or harness design, while one that retains this procedural knowledge can succeed with a weaker model. Memory benchmarks that do not test environment-specific procedural recall are not measuring the capability that determines production reliability.

- **Multi-agent hallucination propagation introduces a communication topology variable that current orchestration frameworks have not accounted for.** The collective hallucination model [8] shows that peer-to-peer coordination patterns — debate, consensus — maximize hallucination amplification precisely because they maximize inter-agent information exchange. Trajel's taxonomy [9] independently establishes that 48.7% of hallucinated trajectories involve multiple simultaneous types, meaning binary detectors fail systematically on the most dangerous compound cases. Together, these findings surface a tradeoff that is invisible to current orchestration frameworks: coordination modes selected for reasoning quality may also be selecting for hallucination propagation risk. The enterprise MCP governance architecture proposed in Queen-Bee [7] addresses policy enforcement and tenant isolation but does not yet close this amplification gap, which is structurally prior to protocol-level controls.
