# Enterprise GenAI Adoption — March 21, 2026

**Area:** Enterprise GenAI Adoption  
**Date:** 2026-03-21  
**Sources:** NVIDIA State of AI 2026, EY Technology Pulse Poll (Feb 2026), Deloitte State of AI 2026, Futurum Group, IBM IBV

---

## The State of Play

Enterprise AI has crossed a threshold. Adoption is no longer the main challenge — **execution is**. Deloitte's *State of AI 2026* report puts it plainly: most enterprises are investing and planning to scale, but operational pressure is the new bottleneck. The question has shifted from "should we do this?" to "why aren't we getting more out of it?"

NVIDIA's annual industry survey (3,200+ responses across financial services, retail/CPG, healthcare, telecom, and manufacturing) confirms the momentum:

- **64%** of enterprises are actively using AI in operations (up from prior years)
- **28%** are still in the assessment phase; just **8%** have no AI plans
- **76%** of large enterprises (1,000+ employees) report active AI usage, with only **2%** not using AI at all
- North America leads at **70%** active adoption; EMEA at 65%; APAC at 63%

The gap between leaders and laggards is widening fast. Leaders are deploying gen AI in under three months; slower organizations remain stuck in pilot mode indefinitely.

---

## Where ROI Is Landing

The industries generating the clearest returns are financial services, retail/CPG, and healthcare/life sciences. Common themes across sectors:

- **Customer-facing and content-heavy use cases** deliver the fastest ROI — personalization, summarisation, customer service automation
- IBM research finds product development teams following top AI practices report a **median genAI ROI of 55%**
- McKinsey data: personalized marketing via AI can reduce customer acquisition costs by up to 50%, boost revenues 5–15%, and improve marketing ROI by 10–30%
- GenAI in R&D is projected to deliver **10–15% expense savings**

The **2026 spending priority shift** is telling: 42% of organizations say *optimizing AI workflows and production cycles* is their #1 spend priority this year — not new pilots, but squeezing more out of existing deployments.

## The Velocity Paradox (EY, Feb 2026)

EY surveyed 500 US tech industry leaders in February 2026. The findings are a useful counterweight to the optimism:

- **97%** view autonomous AI as "high" or "essential" to long-term competitive strategy
- **85%** prioritize speed-to-market over exhaustive AI vetting
- **52%** of department-level AI initiatives are operating **without formal approval or oversight**
- **78%** of leaders say adoption is outpacing their ability to manage business risk
- **45%** experienced a confirmed or suspected sensitive data leak in the last 12 months due to unauthorized GenAI tool use
- **39%** report confirmed or suspected proprietary IP leaks

EY frames this as the "velocity paradox" — urgency versus accountability. Without governance, companies risk hitting a plateau where large-scale transformation stalls.

Despite risks, **95%** of tech executives plan to increase AI spending in the next year. Top destinations: cybersecurity (79%), cloud (67%), AI-specific talent (65%), compute/infra (62%).

## Vendor Landscape: Agentic AI Goes Mainstream

The enterprise platform race has converged on agentic AI as the next competitive axis:

**ServiceNow:** *Now Assist* passed **$600M in annual contract value**. The new **AI Control Tower** allows enterprises to govern and orchestrate fleets of AI agents — both native and third-party — through a unified interface. Autonomous Workforce GA is slated for Q2 2026, with claims of 99% faster IT case resolution. ServiceNow is positioning itself as the orchestration layer across the enterprise stack.

**Salesforce:** Q4 FY2026 earnings beat driven by agentic AI adoption. Agentforce (their agentic platform) is generating material enterprise contracts, particularly in sales, service, and marketing automation.

**Nasdaq (case study):** Built an internal AI platform on NVIDIA infrastructure to unify operations across its diverse businesses. Quote from SVP Michael O'Rourke: *"AI will help bring together data from all our different businesses and products."*

**Open source:** A consistent throughline across surveys — enterprises are increasingly building with open-source models rather than relying solely on proprietary APIs. Gives control over costs, data, and fine-tuning.

## Maturity Stages: Where Most Organizations Are Stuck

Research from Gloat segments enterprise AI maturity into four stages:

1. **Stage 1** — Modest productivity gains from isolated tools (where most orgs sit today)
2. **Stage 2** — Workflow integration; productivity scales across teams
3. **Stage 3** — Breakthrough productivity; AI embedded in decision-making
4. **Stage 4** — AI-native operating model; autonomous functions

By 2026, leading companies are progressing to Stages 3 and 4. The majority remain at Stage 1 — achieving individual efficiency gains but not systemic transformation. The differentiator: infrastructure, governance, and auditability resolved *before* deployment, not after.

## Implications for Scott

1. **The "adoption is solved" narrative is half-true.** Adoption rates look great in surveys; actually getting value from production deployments is the hard part. Anyone selling you adoption numbers as success metrics is obscuring what matters.

2. **Governance debt is accumulating fast.** EY's finding that 52% of department-level initiatives lack oversight is a preview of what the next wave of enterprise AI headlines will look like — data breaches, IP leaks, compliance failures. This connects directly to the Safety & Governance area.

3. **ServiceNow's AI Control Tower is worth tracking.** If it delivers on the promise of cross-platform agent orchestration with governance baked in, it could become the enterprise AI control plane — a genuinely interesting architectural shift.

4. **Open source as enterprise strategy** is no longer contrarian. Regulated industries in particular are moving this way to control data residency and reduce vendor lock-in.
